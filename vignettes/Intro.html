Introduction: methodologies and implementation

Published

June 4, 2026

The comprehensivecohort package provides tools for analyzing data from comprehensive cohort studies in the presence of unmeasured confounding and missing outcome (either continuous or binary). The goal is to estimate the comprehensive cohort causal effects (CCCE), the average causal effect for the entire cohort. This vignette has two parts: 1). Methodologies. This part will cover details on what is Comprehensive Cohort Study design, the causal estimands, assumptions, and brief descriptions of the estimation process. 2). Implementation. This part will focus on how to use the R functions to implement the estimation of CCCE under pre-specified sensitivity parameters. We will also provide codes for presenting results.

Methodologies

Comprehensive Cohort Study design

The Comprehensive Cohort Study (CCS) design is a nested study design where individuals can opt to participate in an randomized control trial (RCT) or the parallel observational study (OBS). In RCT, individuals are randomized to treatments. However, in OBS, individuals can choose the treatment options provided in RCT. All individuals are followed-up. Thus, baseline covariates, treatment and outcome information are collected for the entire cohort.

Causal estimands

Let \(X\) be baseline covariates, \(R\) randomization consent indicator (\(1\) for RCT, \(0\) for OBS), \(T\) binary treatment indicator, \(Y\) as outcome and \(M\) the missing outcome indicator (\(M=1\) if \(Y\) is observed, \(M=0\) if \(Y\) is missing). Let \(O = (X, R, T, Y)\) be the full data without outcome missingness, and \(\widetilde{O} = (X,R,T,M,Y:M=1)\) the observed data. Let \(P\) and \(\widetilde{P}\) be the distribution of \(O\) and \(\widetilde{O}\).

We further introduce the following notations:

  • \(\widetilde{F}_{t, r}(y|x)=P(Y\leq y\mid T=t, R=r, X=x, M=1)\)
  • \(\widetilde{\mu}_{t,r}(h(Y); x)=E[h(Y) \mid T=t, R=r, X=x, M=1]\)
  • \(\pi_{t,r}(x)=P(T = t \mid R=r, X=x)\)
  • \(g_r(x)=P(R = r \mid X=x)\)
  • \(\eta_m(x, r, t)=P(M =m \mid X=x, R=r, T=t)\)

Our causal estimand of interest is the comprehensive cohort causal effect (CCCE): \(E[Y(1)]-E[Y(0)]\). We will draw inference about \(E[Y(t)]\).

Assumptions

  • Consistency: \(Y(t) = Y\) if \(T=t\), for \(t=0,1\).
  • Positivity: \(0 < P(T=1 \mid R=1) < 1\) and \(0 < P(T=1 \mid R=0, X=x) < 1\) for all \(x\) in the support of \(X\).
  • Randomization in RCT: \(T \perp \{ Y(t), X \} \mid R=1\), for \(t = 0, 1\).
  • Confounding Controlled by \(\gamma_t\) in OBS: \[dF(y(t) \mid T=1-t,R=0,X) = dF(y(t) \mid T=t,R=0,X) \times \frac{ \exp\{ \gamma_t s_t(y(t)) \} }{ \mu_{t, 0} (\exp\{ \gamma_t s_t(Y(t)) \}; X)}\]
  • Missing-At-Random Assumption for Outcome: \(M \perp Y \mid T, X, R\).

Under the assumptions, we can identify \(E[Y(t)]\) as

\[\begin{aligned} \psi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t) =& \underbrace{E[ \widetilde{\mu}_{t,1}(Y;X) \big| R=1]}_{\psi_{t,1}(\widetilde{P})} P(R=1)\\ &+ \underbrace{E \bigg[ \widetilde{\mu}_{t,0}(Y;X) \pi_{t,0}(X) +\frac{ \widetilde{\mu}_{t,0}(Y \! \exp\{ \gamma_t s_t(Y)\} ;X) }{\widetilde{\mu}_{t,0}( \exp\{ \gamma_t s_t(Y)\} ;X) } \pi_{1-t,0}(X) \bigg| R=0\bigg]}_{\psi_{t,0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)} P(R=0) \end{aligned}\]

\(\psi_{t, 1}(\widetilde{P})\) and \(\psi_{t, 0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)\) are the identifications of \(E[Y(t)|R=1]\) and \(E[Y(t)|R=0]\). Details of estimations for \(E[Y(t)|R=1]\) and \(E[Y(t)|R=0]\) can be found in the paper. We define the randomized trial causal effect (RTCE) as \(\mbox{RTCE}=\psi_{1, 1}(\widetilde{P})-\psi_{0, 1}(\widetilde{P})\); and the patient preference causal effect (PPCE) as \(\mbox{PPCE}(\gamma_1,\gamma_0) = \psi_{1, 0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_1)-\psi_{0, 0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_0)\).

Methods

We will describe the main estimation steps for \(\psi_{t}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)\): (1) Derive the efficient influence function. (2) Estimate \(\widetilde{P}\). (3) Compute the one-step, truncated, split sample estimator. (4) Compute the variance and \(95\%\) confidence interval.

(1). Derive the efficient influence function for \(\psi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)\), denoted \(\phi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)(\widetilde{O})\)

\[\phi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)(\widetilde{O}) = \upsilon_{t, 1}(\widetilde{P})(\widetilde{O})\times P(R=1)+\upsilon_{t, 0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)(\widetilde{O})\times P(R=0) - \psi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t) \ , \]

where the exact form of \(\upsilon_{t, 1}(\widetilde{P})(\widetilde{O})\) and \(\upsilon_{t, 0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)(\widetilde{O})\) can be found in the paper.

(2). Estimate \(\widetilde{P}\)

To construct a estimator for \(E[Y(t)]\), we need to estimate \(\pi_{t, r}(X)\), \(\eta_1(X, r, t)\) and \(\widetilde{F}_{t, r}(y|X)\) for \(r=0, 1\). The estimation methods we apply have fast enough convergence rate so that our resulting estimators converge to a normal distribution at \(\sqrt{n}\)-rates. Specifically, we apply the generalized additive model (GAM) with a logistic link function (Hastie et al. 1990) for \(\pi_{t, r}(X)\) and \(\eta_1(X, r, t)\) for \(r=0, 1\). We apply the single index model (Chiang and Huang 2012) for \(\widetilde{F}_{t, r}(y|X)\) for \(r=0, 1\). We then estimate \(\widetilde{\mu}_{t, 0}\big(Y\exp(\gamma_t s_t( Y)); X\big)\), \(\widetilde{\mu}_{t, 0}\big(\exp(\gamma_t s_t(Y)); X\big)\) and \(\widetilde{\mu}_{t, r}\big(Y; X\big)\) by numeric integration.

(3). Compute the one-step, truncated, split sample estimator

We apply K-fold sample splitting and tuning-free Huberization procedure (Wang et al. 2021) to truncate the k-th split \(\upsilon_{t, 1} \left(\widehat{\widetilde{P}}^{(-k)}\right) (\widetilde{O}_i)\) and \(\upsilon_{t, 0}\left(\widehat{\widetilde{P}}^{(-k)} ; \gamma_t\right) (\widetilde{O}_i)\):

\[\begin{equation*} \widehat{\psi}^\dagger_t(\gamma_t) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \underbrace{\left\{\frac{1}{n_k} \sum_{i: S_i=k}\Bigg[ \underbrace{\upsilon_{t, 1}^\dagger \left(\widehat{\widetilde{P}}^{(-k)}\right) (\widetilde{O}_i)\times P_{n/n_k}^{(-k)}(R=1) +\upsilon_{t, 0}^\dagger \left(\widehat{\widetilde{P}}^{(-k)} ; \gamma_t\right) (\widetilde{O}_i)\times P_{n/n_k}^{(-k)}(R=0)}_{\upsilon_t^\dagger\left(\widehat{\widetilde{P}}^{(-k)} ; \gamma_t\right)(\widetilde{O}_i)}\Bigg]\right\}}_{\widehat{\psi}_t^{(k)\dagger}(\gamma_t)} \end{equation*}\] where \(S_i\) is the split membership of the \(i\)th observation (i.e, \(S_i \in \{1, \ldots, K\}\)).

(4). Compute variance and \(95\%\) confidence interval

We estimate the variance of \(\widehat{\psi}_t^\dagger(\gamma_t)\) by \[\sigma_t^{\dagger 2}=\frac{1}{nK}\sum_{k=1}^K\left\{\frac{1}{n_k-1}\sum_{i: S_i=k}\left\{\upsilon_t^\dagger\left(\widehat{\widetilde{P}}^{(-k)} ; \gamma_t\right)(\widetilde{O}_i)-\widehat{\psi}_t^{(k)\dagger}(\gamma_t)\right\}^2\right\}\]

The \(95\%\) Wald confidence interval for \(\psi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)\) is \(\widehat{\psi}^\dagger_t(\gamma_t)\pm z_{0.975}\frac{\sigma_t^\dagger}{\sqrt{n}}\).

Implementation

We will demonstrate how to use the R functions in comprehensivecohort to estimate CCCE, using an example data included in the package. First, install package directly from git:

remotes::install_github("UofUEpiBio/Comprehensive_cohort")

Data

The dataset included in the package is a simulated data based on the TOIB study (Underwood et al. 2008). The TOIB study is a comprehensive cohort study on the effect of topical versus oral non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) in managing knee pain among older adults with chronic knee pain. In the analysis, the outcome of interest is Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index (WOMAC) pain score (\(0-100\)) at 12 months. We include age, baseline WOMAC pain score, expected pain a year from now and chronic pain grade as baseline covariates.

The dataset has 563 observations and 8 variables, which include:

  • Y: WOMAC pain score at 12 months, continuous variable ranging from 0 to 100. NA indicate missing outcome.
  • M: outcome missingness indicator, binary variable. \(1\) if \(Y\) is observed, \(0\) if \(Y\) is missing.
  • R: randomization consent indicator, binary variable. \(1\) for RCT, \(0\) for OBS.
  • t: treatment indicator, binary variable. \(1\) for topical NSAIDs treatment group, \(0\) for oral NSAIDs treatment group.
  • age: age, continuous variable.
  • womac_bq: WOMAC pain score at baseline, continuous variable ranging from 0 to 100. No missing value.
  • expectationb: expected pain a year from now, categorical variable. Three levels: much/a little worse, about the same, a little/much better/free of pain.
  • ChronicPainb: chronic pain grade, categorical variable. Two levels: 1-2, 3-4.

Application

The main function is est_psi(), which outputs point estimates, variance and \(95\%\) Walds confidence interval for \(\psi_t(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)\), \(\psi_{t, 0}(\widetilde{P}; \gamma_t)\) and \(\psi_{t, 1}(\widetilde{P})\) under one or more pre-specified \(\gamma_t\).

Users need to input data and specify several parameters:

  • Data: Y, M, R, t, M (baseline covariates in the form of data frame)
  • Estimand of interest: trt=1 if estimating \(E[Y(1)]\), \(E[Y(1)|R=0]\) and \(E[Y(1)|R=1]\); trt=0 if estimating \(E[Y(0)]\), \(E[Y(0)|R=0]\) and \(E[Y(0)|R=1]\)
  • Sensitivity parameters: gamma, a vector of \(\gamma_t\) value.
  • Single index model settings (Redd et al. 2025): kernel, kernel smoothing, choices of Gaussian and Epanechnikov kernel; single_index_method, types of constraints in estimation, choices of setting first coefficient to 1, norm of coefficients to 1, and bandwidth to 1; method, optimization methods, default to optim; use_mave, whether to apply MAVE (Xia et al. 2002; Wang and Xia 2008) or cumulative sliced inverse regression method (Zhu et al. 2010) to estimate initial value of the coeffficients, default to TRUE.
  • Truncation methods: simple_trunc=TRUE to apply quantile truncation of \(\frac{1}{\pi_{t, r}(X)}\) and \(\frac{1}{\eta_m(X, r, t)}\), simple_trunc=FALSE to apply tuning-free Huberization procedure (Wang et al. 2021) to influence functions. If simple_trunc=TRUE, specify quantile truncation by setting quant from 0 to 1.
  • K-fold sample splitting: fold=K, a integer. seed, set.seed(seed).
## data adaptive truncation
out <- with(ccohort, {
  est_psi(Y, M, R, X = data.frame(age, womac_bq, expectationb, ChronicPainb), 
          t, trt = 1, gamma = c(0, 0.5), fold = 5, seed = 1, IF_output = FALSE,
          simple_trunc = FALSE, quant = NULL, kernel="dnorm", 
          single_index_method="norm1coef", method="optim")
})
